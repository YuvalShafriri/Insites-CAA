In vain, great-hearted Kublai, shall I attempt to describe to you the city of Zaira, with its high bastions.
I could tell you how many steps make up the streets that are like ladders, what kind of zinc plates cover the roofs;
but I already know that this would be the same as telling you nothing.
The city is not made of these, but of relationships between the measurements of its space and the events of its past: the distance from the ground to the street lamp and the dangling feet of a hanged usurper;
the wire stretched from the street lamp to the railing of the opposite balcony and the ribbons that decorated the route of the queen's bridal procession;
the height of the railing and the leap of the lover who jumps over it at dawn; the tilt of a gutter and the progress of a cat walking along it to slip into that same window;
the firing trajectory of the gunboat that suddenly appeared and the shell that destroyed the gutter; the tears in the fishing nets and the three old men sitting on the dock repairing the nets and at the same time telling each other for the hundredth time the story of the gunboat of the usurper, who is said to have been the illegitimate son of the queen and her lover, abandoned in his swaddling clothes right there on the dock.
Absorbing this flowing wave of memories, the city breathes like a sponge and expands. A description of Zaira as it is today would have to contain all of its past.
However, the city does not tell its past, but contains it like the lines of a hand, written in the street corners, the window grilles, the banisters of the stairways, the lightning rods, the flagpoles, every segment marked in its turn with scratches, indentations, cuts, and notches.
