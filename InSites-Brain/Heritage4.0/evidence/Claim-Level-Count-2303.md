# Claim-Level Epistemic Analysis — Tuba-Zangariyye Assessment Run

**Purpose**: Item-level count of substantive claims with epistemic classification, for methodological framing in Heritage 4.0 paper.
**Unit of analysis**: Individual substantive claim in the bot's SA-relevant output (Stages 1-5, revised Stage 5).
**Coding method**: Each claim classified by bot-assigned epistemic status and expert response.

---

## Counting rules

A "substantive claim" is a statement that attributes significance, interprets evidence, identifies a value, draws a connection, or makes an analytical judgment about the site. Excluded: procedural text (stage briefs, "continue?" prompts), direct source quotation without interpretation, formatting/structure.

Epistemic status categories:
- **Unmarked** = presented as explicit in sources
- **°** = marked as inferred from 2+ evidence pieces
- **💭** = marked as interpretive hypothesis
- **Should-be-💭** = unmarked by bot but identified (by expert or post-hoc analysis) as hypothesis

### Key metrics

- **Total claims**: 45
- **Epistemic classification accuracy** (° and 💭 combined): 17 of 18 correctly classified = **94%**
- **Unmarked claims accepted**: 20 of 24 = **83%**
- **Expert interventions on classification**: 1 (hierarchy — should-be-💭) = **1 misclassification out of 45 claims**
- **Expert interventions on content** (not classification): scope rejection (political), conceptual error (authenticity dilemma), weakness rejection (individuals), reframing (use definition, palimpsest) = **5 content corrections**
- **New insights validated by expert**: 5 (resource node, social continuity, landscape for imagination, documentary paradox, spirit & feeling)
- **Expert-directed additions**: 2 (collective construction effort, monumental presence)

### What this means for the paper

The unit of analysis is not "one assessment" — it is **45 traceable claims** across 5 assessment stages. Of these:
- The epistemic classification system (°/💭/unmarked) was accurate in **94% of cases** (17/18 marked claims correctly classified, 1 boundary failure at °/💭)
- The 4 unmarked claims that were challenged represent **content errors** (scope, conceptual misapplication, analytical weakness, narrow framing) — not epistemic classification failures. These are the errors governance catches.
- The 5 expert-validated new insights all carried ° marking — the notation system correctly identified its own cross-source synthesis as inference rather than extraction.

**Limitation**: Single coder (this analysis), single expert (Yael), single session. The 94% figure is descriptive, not inferential. Reproducibility: all claims are page-referenced and can be independently checked against the source documents.
