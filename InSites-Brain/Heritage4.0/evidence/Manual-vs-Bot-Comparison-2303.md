# Manual vs. AI-Assisted Assessment Comparison
## Tuba-Zangariyye Dolmen Field — Same Site, Same Expert, Same Sources

**Manual**: EAC Guidance 11, site 7.12 (formal CBSA by Yael Alef, standard practice, no AI)
**AI-assisted**: InSites-CAA v2 with HITL governance (Claude Opus, same expert, same 3 source documents)

### Critical data relationship
The source data provided to the bot **includes the descriptive sections written by Yael for the EAC11 case** (Source B = Alef, Mordohovich & Berger), plus some additional descriptive evidence from the essay that feeds into the SA but is not assessment itself. The significance assessment (SA) sections were withheld. This means:
- The bot re-read **Yael's own descriptions** + two additional sources (A: survey, C: Stepansky synthesis)
- The new insights the bot produced came from re-processing text the expert herself had written — not from new data
- The comparison is therefore: **same descriptions → different significance outputs** — the difference is the structured workflow with cross-source synthesis, not access to different information

### Comparison scope
This comparison addresses **only the significance assessment (SA) outputs** — values identified, analytical framing, and significance synthesis. The EAC11 essay contains additional sections (management context, conservation impact, decision outcomes) that are not part of the SA and are not compared here. The AI-assisted run also produces outputs beyond SA (KG, dashboard, session report) noted separately as structural additions.

## 6. Bottom Line for the Paper

### What the comparison demonstrates

**The AI-assisted workflow expanded the assessment in three directions the manual did not reach:**
1. **Cross-source synthesis** — connected data scattered across documents, sections, and footnotes into interpretive frames (4 expert-validated new insights)
2. **Explicit epistemic structure** — made uncertainty visible, distinguished extraction from inference from hypothesis
3. **Analytical infrastructure** — KG, dashboard, session report, evidence tagging create reusable outputs for future assessment, research, and management

**The manual SA had its own strengths:**
1. **Direct compression** — the significance judgment is stated concisely without requiring the reader to follow a multi-stage reasoning chain
2. **Implicit expert judgment** — values, framing, and scope reflect decades of domain expertise without needing to be externalized or corrected

**The key finding is not that one is better.** It is that they produce *different kinds of output* from the *same inputs*:
- The manual compresses expert judgment into actionable conclusions
- The AI-assisted workflow externalizes the reasoning process, makes it traceable, and catches cross-source connections the expert didn't notice — but also requires the expert to catch what the AI gets wrong

**For the Heritage 4.0 paper**: This comparison is unusually clean. The bot re-read the expert's own descriptions and — through structured cross-source synthesis under uncertainty notation — surfaced insights the expert had not reached from her own text. The new insights are not from new data; they are from a different *process* applied to the same data. This directly supports the paper's core argument: the governed AI-assisted workflow opens new lines of inquiry not because the AI knows more, but because the structured methodology (context-effect ontology + uncertainty notation + HITL) activates a different kind of reading — cross-referencing, pattern-connecting, footnote-elevating — that complements the expert's own domain-driven reading.

At the same time, the 13 expert interventions show that the workflow depends on the expert's judgment to function. The architecture's value is not replacing the manual process but producing a richer assessment through governed collaboration — same descriptions, different (and complementary) significance outputs.
