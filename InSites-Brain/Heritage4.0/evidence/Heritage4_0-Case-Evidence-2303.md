## 3. Demonstration A — Sub-textual Insight (° notation as epistemic enabler)

The paper's core argument: uncertainty notation licenses the AI to search for patterns beyond the explicit text, because these outputs are labeled as inferences for expert examination, not presented as facts.

Four instances where the bot synthesized across sources to produce insights not stated in any single document:

### 3.1 Geographic reframing: "resource node in a landscape corridor"

**What the sources say:**
- Source A (p.1): "the spring of ʿEn Zifan rises to the surface... Three other springs are located near the spur: ʿEn ʿAdaya, about 1 km to its west, and ʿEn Tovim and ʿEn Ha-Nasi, about 0.7 km to its east" — factual location markers
- Source C (pp.40-41): describes the plateau as sustaining "permanent sedentary settlement, mostly – but not necessarily – adjacent to the water sources, with an economy based mainly on local field cultivation and/or cattle breeding" — general settlement ecology

**What the bot produced (Stage 1, Geographic context):**
> "This position — on a water-rich plateau straddling two major geographical basins — frames the field as occupying a resource node within a landscape corridor that has sustained both sedentary and mobile populations since at least the Chalcolithic period."

**Analysis:**
- No source connects the four springs as a spatial pattern framing the site
- No source uses "resource node" or "landscape corridor" — these are the bot's interpretive synthesis
- The inference combines A's geographic specifics with C's settlement-ecology discussion
- Correctly marked ° — inferred from combining evidence across sources

**Expert response (from observation rubric):**
> "Amazed by the new insight that was not written anywhere before. It strengthens the meaning of 'cultural landscape' — the connecting nature and human activity."

**Planning implication (bot-generated, expert-validated):** Include springs and wadis in any development buffer — "a new idea not written anywhere."

### 3.2 Social continuity: 4000-year pastoralist arc

**What the sources say:**
- Source C (pp.46-48): links dolmen builders to IB-MBIIA semi-nomadic pastoralists via Horbat Berekh settlement finds (animal pens, flint sickles, grinding stones)
- Source C (footnote 4, p.50): "This mode of life has been discerned as the main character of Galilee-Bedouin 'Nomadism' in the 19th century... and until recently was the mainstay of the Zangariya and El-Heib tribes who have been living on the Corazim Plateau since the 18th century at least"
- Source B: mentions Bedouin grazing the area and development threatening the landscape

**What the bot produced (Stage 1, Social context):**
> "This social continuity — pastoralist populations using the same landscape over four millennia — frames the dolmen field not merely as Bronze Age mortuary architecture but as an enduring node in a pastoral territory."

**Analysis:**
- Source C's main text discusses ancient pastoralists (pp.46-48); the modern Bedouin connection is in footnote 4 — separated by several pages and different sections
- Source B mentions Bedouin in a management context, not as a heritage-continuity thread
- No source traces the full arc (Bronze Age builders → Ottoman cultivators → modern Bedouin) as a single narrative of landscape continuity
- The bot connected data scattered across different documents and sections into a coherent interpretive frame

**Expert response:**
> "Pattern identification I didn't identify... turned the focus from the burial structures to the economy of those who built the dolmens — and the connection to the Bedouin"

### 3.3 "Landscape for imagination" — persistent cultural meaning

**What the sources say:**
- Source C (footnote 2, p.50): "The Dolmens may have nurtured the biblical tradition of the shadowy giant-people who are labeled in the Old Testament 'Rephaim'... The passage in Deuteronomy 3:11... probably refers to a basalt-constructed chamber-tomb Dolmen... The Dolmens are also probably referred to in the New Testament as 'tombs' where outcasts from the surrounding towns lived and took shelter (Mark Chap. V)... the native Bedouin of the area call the Dolmens of this area 'Dan', a term meaning 'shelter'... During Late Roman times, the Jewish populace saw them as Pagan entities, and they are referred to in the Talmud as 'Merkolis'"

**What the bot produced (Stage 1, Intangible Heritage context):**
> "These layers of intangible association — pre-Israelite myth, Christian narrative, rabbinic prohibition, and Bedouin oral tradition — frame the dolmens as generators of cultural meaning far beyond their original funerary function... a persistent landscape for imagination."

**Analysis:**
- All raw data comes from a single footnote in Source C
- Source C treats these as scholarly notes on textual references — not as a unified interpretive theme
- The bot elevated footnote material into an intangible heritage value: the idea that the physical structures persistently provoke narrative production across unrelated cultural traditions
- "Landscape for imagination" — the phrase itself — is the bot's coinage

**Expert response:**
> "New beautiful insight that was not written before"

### 3.4 Documentary paradox: unexcavated = preservation

**What the sources say:**
- Source A (throughout): treats the unexcavated status as a limitation — "No excavation has been conducted within the Tuba-Zangariyye field itself"
- Source C (p.49): "a final absolute dating for their construction can be attained only in the wake of additional controlled excavations" — frames lack of excavation as a research deficit

**What the bot produced (Stage 3, Nara Grid — Documentary/Archival):**
> "the dolmens have never been excavated — which is, paradoxically, also a form of preservation"

And in the integrity narrative:
> "integrity-as-potential: High — the sealed dolmen chambers, burial fills, and associated deposits remain undisturbed... a finite and non-renewable resource"

**Analysis:**
- Both sources frame the unexcavated status negatively (gap, limitation, deficit)
- The bot reframed it as a positive heritage attribute — the intact deposits are themselves a primary record
- This is a genuine analytical inversion: from "we don't know because we haven't dug" to "what we haven't dug is itself worth preserving"

**Expert response:**
> "A point I didn't develop in the previous assessment without the bot"

## 4. Demonstration B — Hypothesis Generation (💭 notation)

### 4.1 "Hierarchically organized network" (mixed B + C — hypothesis generated, marking failed)

**What the sources say:**
- Source A (p.3): "The concentration of dolmens identified in the present survey is outstanding in both the number and density compared with other dolmen sites on the Korazim Plateau"
- Source A (p.3): "The central location of this dolmen field and the advantageous vistas it offers of other dolmen clusters... indicate that it was an important dolmen site"
- Source A (p.3): "It is possible that the site and the individuals interred within it were of special significance... Both dolmen sites **may have** functioned as significant regional cultural centers"

**What the bot produced (Stage 1, Thematic context):**
> "The identification of such a center at Tuba, in turn, reframes the Shamir and Golan fields as parts of a hierarchically organized network rather than an undifferentiated scatter."

**Analysis:**
- Source A uses "it is possible" and "may have" — hedged language about two peer sites
- Source A describes differentiation (some sites denser, more central) but not hierarchy as an organizational principle
- "Hierarchically organized network" is the bot's interpretive framing — structural language not present in any source
- This should have been marked 💭 — the hypothesis generation is interesting (differentiation → hierarchy?) but the epistemic classification failed

**Expert challenge:**
> "Is this framing a hypothesis of the bot or clearly written in the sources provided?"

**Bot's honest response when challenged:**
> "The phrase 'hierarchically organized network' is my interpretive framing — it goes beyond what the sources state. The sources suggest differentiation but not hierarchy as an organizational principle, and 'network' as a structured system is not their language either."

**Paper value:** This is stronger than a clean success. It shows:
1. The bot can generate interesting hypotheses (differentiation → hierarchy)
2. The bot's own epistemic classification fails (no 💭 marking)
3. The expert catches the failure through domain knowledge
4. The bot traces its reasoning honestly when challenged
5. The governance architecture works precisely when the AI's judgment fails

## 5. Demonstration C — Prevented Over-claiming

### 5.1 "Authenticity dilemma" (conceptual misapplication)

**What the sources say:** No source discusses authenticity dilemmas.

**What the bot produced (Stage 3):**
> "The principal authenticity dilemma is the tension between the unexcavated archive and the development plan."

**Expert challenge:** "What do you mean by 'authenticity dilemma'?"

**Bot's correction:**
> "I conflated two different things. An authenticity dilemma in the Nara Grid sense is a tension between dimensions of authenticity... What I described — the tension between the unexcavated archive and the development plan — is not an authenticity dilemma. It's a conservation threat."

**Paper value:** The expert's domain knowledge caught a category error that no notation system can detect. This was not a ° vs. 💭 issue — it was a misuse of a technical term from conservation theory. Shows that governance handles failures beyond the scope of uncertainty notation.

### 5.2 "Individuals of special significance" (amplified speculation)

**What the sources say:**
- Source A (p.3): "It is possible that the site and the individuals interred within it were of special significance among the dolmen builders"

**What the bot produced (Stage 5, Significance Statement):**
> "The density and proposed centrality of the Tuba field, in turn, may suggest that the individuals interred here held particular significance among the dolmen-building population"

**Expert response:** "This interpretation is too weak and does not contribute to the statement."

**Paper value:** Even when the source itself speculates ("it is possible"), the bot should not amplify weak speculation into a significance statement. The expert filtered not just for accuracy but for analytical contribution — a weak claim weakens the whole statement.

### 5.3 Political context included (non-CBSA content)

**What the sources say:**
- Source B: 2 pages on IAA management process, Antiquities Law, Valletta Convention, salvage excavation policy

**What the bot did:** Included a full "Political context" paragraph in Stage 1.

**Expert response:** "Not relevant to this assessment" — the content describes the case study's professional significance, not the site's cultural significance.

**Paper value:** The bot doesn't distinguish between heritage-relevant and administratively-relevant content in source material. Source presence ≠ CBSA relevance. This supports the paper's argument that expert governance directs analytical scope, not just corrects errors.

## 7. What the Bot Missed (governance as attention direction)

Content present in the sources that the bot did not incorporate:

| Missed content | Source | Why it matters for the paper |
|---------------|--------|------------------------------|
| Khirbat Tuba: Chalcolithic pottery, Roman/Byzantine/Ottoman settlement, Bronze Age II figurine | A (p.2) | Would have enriched the multi-period picture — shows bot's synthesis is selective |
| Corbelling as possible prototype for Canaanite tomb construction at Ugarit, Megiddo, Dan | C (p.44) | A provocative 💭 the bot should have generated — a research question explicitly posed by the source |
| Chamber orientation: 60% face easterly directions (shared with Golan) | C (p.43) | Ritual/cultural significance data the bot overlooked |
| "Es-Shegerat el-Mubarakat" (The Blessed Trees) — Bedouin name for Horbat Berekh area | C (p.48) | Intangible heritage detail that would have strengthened the "landscape for imagination" theme |
| Stepansky's core question: "what stimulated the Dolmen builders into investing so much of their limited resources into Megalithic Tomb construction?" | C (p.49) | The central research enigma of the site — should have been a thread in the significance statement |

**Paper argument this supports:** Expert governance is not just error correction — it is attention direction. The bot's cross-source synthesis catches some connections but misses others. The expert brings research intuition about which connections matter, which questions are central, which details carry interpretive weight. This cannot be automated.
